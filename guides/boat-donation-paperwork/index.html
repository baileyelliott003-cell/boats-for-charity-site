Boat Donation Paperwork: Complete Document Checklist

Organize boat, trailer, lien, estate, marina, transfer, acknowledgment, and tax records.

The short answer

The paperwork for a boat donation falls into six files: proof of the boat's identity, proof of your authority to transfer it, anything showing liens or unpaid balances, the trailer's own documents, the transfer records you create at handover, and the tax records you keep afterward. Get those in order and a donation moves cleanly. Miss one, most often the trailer or a lien, and it stalls. Requirements differ by state, so treat this as a working checklist and confirm the specifics with your state boating or titling agency and DMV. A complete file speeds an individual review; it does not by itself promise acceptance, pickup, timing, or a tax outcome.

Why paperwork is the whole game

A boat is legal property before it is anything else. When it changes hands, the record of who owned it and who owns it now has to be clean, or problems surface later for both sides. Good paperwork protects you: it shows you transferred the boat, ended your liability, and, when a deduction applies, backs up your tax position. It also lets a review move quickly, because the facts are already documented instead of reconstructed by phone. The rest of this guide walks through the six files in the order you will usually assemble them.

File 1 — Boat identity

Establish exactly which vessel this is. Gather the title, the current registration, and federal documentation if the boat is documented with the U.S. Coast Guard National Vessel Documentation Center. Add a clear photo of the hull identification number, the official number if there is one, the builder's plate, and any prior bill of sale. These are what let anyone match the physical boat to its records.

File 2 — Owner authority

Confirm the person signing is the person entitled to sign. Match the printed owner name. Co-owners, companies, trusts, estates, powers of attorney, divorce decrees, and guardianships each add documents, because in those cases the title alone does not establish who may transfer the boat.

File 3 — Liens and balances

Money owed against the boat travels with it. Locate lender payoff or lien-release letters, any mechanic's-lien notice, marina or slip balances, storage invoices, unpaid tax records, and any impound notice. Surfacing these early is far better than discovering them mid-transfer.

File 4 — Trailer record

If a trailer is part of the gift, it is effectively a second vehicle. Capture its VIN, its title or registration, the plate, the owner of record, its weight rating, and its condition. The hull's paperwork does not transfer the trailer, and a roadworthy hull does not make an unsafe or undocumented trailer legal to tow.

File 5 — The transfer file

This is what you create at handover and keep. Retain signed title assignments and any bill of sale, receipts, a simple inventory of keys handed over, any notice to the marina or storage yard, your insurance communications, and any liability-release or notice-of-transfer filing your state offers. Many states let a former owner file a release-of-liability notice; doing so is one of the most valuable protective steps available to you.

File 6 — The tax file

Keep the charity's written acknowledgment and any Form 1098-C issued to you, along with your photos, records of the boat's condition, and basis information. Noncash gifts over $500 generally involve IRS Form 8283, and higher-value property may require a qualified appraisal. The governing rules are in IRS Publications 526 and 561; our boat donation tax information guide summarizes them, but confirm your own situation with a qualified tax professional.

How the files come together in practice

Picture a cleanly titled powerboat on its own trailer. The owner pulls the title and registration (File 1), confirms the name matches their ID (File 2), verifies there is no loan outstanding (File 3), and locates the trailer's separate title (File 4). At handover they sign the title assignment, keep a copy, and file a release-of-liability notice with the state (File 5). Afterward they file the acknowledgment with their tax records (File 6). Six files, in order, no surprises.

Now picture an inherited sailboat sitting at a marina. File 2 becomes the hard part: authority runs through the estate, so the executor's appointment documents matter more than the old title. File 3 includes a growing slip balance that has to be addressed. The identity, transfer, and tax files still apply, but the order of effort shifts toward proving authority and clearing the balance first. Same six files; different center of gravity.

Common mistakes to avoid

  • Treating the trailer as an afterthought. It is a separate titled vehicle. Missing trailer paperwork is one of the most common reasons a transfer stalls.
  • Ignoring a lien or balance. A payoff letter or marina invoice does not disappear on transfer. Find and disclose these early.
  • Skipping the release-of-liability filing. If your state offers one, use it. It is your proof that responsibility for the boat ended when you transferred it.
  • Relying on the title alone for an estate or company boat. Those transfers need the underlying authority documents, not just the certificate.
  • Discarding tax records too soon. Keep the acknowledgment, photos, and any Form 8283 or appraisal materials with your return records.

Frequently asked questions

What paperwork do I need to donate a boat?

At a minimum you need proof of who owns the boat and the right to transfer it, the boat's identification numbers, and an accurate description of its condition and location. Depending on the boat you may also need the trailer's title or registration, lien releases, estate or trust documents, and, after the gift, the charity's written acknowledgment. Exact requirements vary by state, so confirm with your state boating or titling agency and DMV.

Do I need the title to donate a boat?

A clear title makes a transfer simpler, but some boats are registration-only, federally documented, or missing a certificate that can be replaced. Whether a boat without a standard title can be donated is decided case by case, and the path to a replacement or bonded title varies by state. See our guide on donating a boat without a title.

Is the trailer covered by the boat's paperwork?

No. The trailer is usually a separate titled or registered vehicle with its own VIN, owner record, and condition and safety questions. Transferring the hull does not transfer the trailer, so gather the trailer's documents separately if a trailer is part of the donation.

What tax documents should I keep after donating?

Keep the charity's written acknowledgment and any Form 1098-C you are issued, plus your own photos and records of the boat's condition and any basis information. Noncash gifts over $500 generally involve IRS Form 8283, and higher-value property may require a qualified appraisal under the rules in IRS Publications 526 and 561. Confirm what applies to you with a qualified tax professional.

How do I handle paperwork for an inherited boat?

Ownership of an inherited boat usually runs through an estate or trust, so the authority to transfer comes from documents like letters testamentary, a trust instrument, or a court appointment rather than the title alone. Gather those alongside any prior title or registration, and confirm the specific steps with the probate court and your state titling agency.
