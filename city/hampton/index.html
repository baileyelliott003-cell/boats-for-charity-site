Boat Donation in Hampton, Virginia

Salt water and slip fees on the lower Chesapeake wear a boat down whether you use her or not, and at some point donating starts to make more sense than another haul-out.

Where most people are starting from

Plenty of the owners who reach out here in Hampton Roads have a boat that hasn't left the slip in a couple of seasons. She still floats off the Hampton River or over toward the mouth of the James, but the bottom needs work, the survey is overdue, and the yard bill keeps landing all the same. Donating is one honest way out, and a good review just starts with the basics: who legally owns her, what shape she's really in, exactly where she sits, and how we'd actually get to her.

None of that guarantees anything. We look at every boat individually, and submitting the form is not a promise of acceptance, pickup, transport, timing, a dollar value, or any particular tax result. It's the start of a conversation, nothing more.

What the water does to a boat here

This is brackish, working water. The lower Chesapeake and Hampton Roads carry a lot of commercial and Navy traffic, the salt is relentless on outdrives and fasteners, and hurricane and nor'easter season means real storm exposure from late summer into fall. Winters are mild enough that many boats stay wet year-round, so tell us when yours last ran and whether she saw any freeze, blow, or flooding.

Photos carry the story better than a description. Shoot every side of the hull, the deck, interior, helm, bilge, engine, the ID plates, and anything that's clearly wrong: blistering, growth, soft spots, corrosion, water sitting where it shouldn't.

Storage and access, plainly

An address tells us almost nothing about whether a boat can actually come out. Between the bridge-tunnels, low fixed spans, and cramped marina yards around the region, access is usually the hard part. Show us the whole path, not just the boat.

In a slip

Marina and dock rules, the slip location, tide and depth at the ramp or lift, how we get keys or gate access, and whether she can move under her own power.

On a trailer

The trailer's plate and frame, tires, hubs, lights, coupler, and bunks, plus the actual route out of the yard and onto the road.

On the hard

Stands and blocking, whether a lift or forklift is needed, ground firmness after rain, gate width, and any yard deadline or vendor-approval rule.

Ownership and paperwork

Match every document to the name and hull number on the boat. Virginia registers many vessels through the Department of Wildlife Resources, while larger boats may be Coast Guard documented, and a trailer carries its own title. Pull together what you can: the hull identification number, registration or official number, owner's name, any lien, the trailer VIN, and any estate, trust, or divorce authority that applies. When something's missing, that's fine, just tell us, and confirm current requirements with the state agency or the Coast Guard's National Vessel Documentation Center where it applies. Our paperwork checklist walks through it, and if the title's gone, start with donating without a title.

Getting her moved is its own question

Transport gets reviewed separately, because beam, weight, height, trailer roadworthiness, haul-out needs, the route, and the destination all matter. Until there's a written plan and the marina confirms its own requirements, keep the boat secured and under your control. Don't drop insurance or give up the slip on the strength of an inquiry.

Questions from Hampton boat owners

Can I submit a non-running boat in Hampton?

Yes. A dead outboard or a seized inboard doesn't disqualify a boat here. Tell us what stopped working, how long she's been sitting, whether she's in a slip or on a trailer, and the current state of the hull and rigging. Every boat is reviewed on its own facts.

What if I can't find the title?

Note what you do have and what's missing. Virginia titles and registers many vessels through the Department of Wildlife Resources, while larger boats may be Coast Guard documented, so the right next step depends on how yours is recorded, any lien, and who the legal owner is.

Is pickup or transport guaranteed?

No. Beam, weight, trailer condition, whether a marina travel-lift is needed, the road out, and the destination all have to be weighed first. Around the bridge-tunnels and tight marina yards of Hampton Roads, access is often the deciding factor.

Should I cancel insurance or my slip right away?

Not yet. Keep coverage and your slip or storage in place until the transfer is actually complete and the marina, insurer, and any agency have gotten whatever notice they require.
